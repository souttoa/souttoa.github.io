title: "LastLeg: Replacing Physical Maps with Low-Friction Directions"
---

For the past year, I have been thinking about how we navigate unfamiliar large public spaces like shopping centers, concert venues, and university campuses. Physical map kiosks sit scattered around locations like these, but people today rely heavily on their phones for directions. Standing in front of a giant directory trying to sort through shops and trace a path feels outdated. Translating a flat static map into your physical surroundings is a process that our devices can easily simplify.

Interestingly, Apple tried to address this gap by [introducing their Indoor Maps program in 2019](https://developer.apple.com/videos/play/wwdc2019/245/) with the goal of bringing indoor navigation directly to iPhones. The process for venues to join, however, is strict. Vendors face high barriers to entry, [limiting the feature to places with massive annual visitorships](https://www.reddit.com/r/applemaps/comments/mzqdba/comment/gw4h4h9/?utm_source=share&utm_medium=web3x&utm_name=web3xcss&utm_term=1&utm_content=share_button). Venues also receive very little dedicated support from Apple once they enroll. Standard GPS signals also fail indoors because satellite data cannot easily penetrate thick concrete roofs or multi-level floors.

I wanted to build a more dedicated alternative that actually makes sense for venues in a compact yet user-friendly package. A practical challenge immediately presented itself: people do not want to download an entire application just to find a specific event room or bathroom. LastLeg solves this by running as an iOS App Clip: you scan a physical code and the interface opens instantly, with your location information. 

I spent a significant amount of time refining the visual experience. Standard Apple view transitions lock you into their layout engine, so I bypassed those constraints by building a custom layout engine in SwiftUI. By using absolute coordinate tracking and keyframes, I choreographed fluid animations throughout the interface.

To keep the application lightweight, I separated the UI layer from the heavy routing math. Processing complex graph theory operations on-device consumes too much memory. Instead, the app sends user coordinates to a GraphHopper server via a REST API. The server computes the shortest path across custom OpenStreetMap nodes I plotted, returning the exact routing data for MapKit to draw. I also wired the app to a live university event database. Using Swift concurrency, the app fetches and decodes JSON payloads in the background so users can search for temporary campus events in real time.

A major focus of the design was making navigation accessible. Many mapping tools assume the user can take any physical path. The app includes features to meet ADA compliance measures by prioritizing routes with ramps and elevators. This specific logic makes moving around easier for people with mobility challenges.

The current version works well outdoors and in simple indoor areas. I plan to introduce precise indoor tracking next by integrating inexpensive iBeacon units throughout the venue to pinpoint a user's location indoors. These small Bluetooth devices provide the exact location accuracy that standard GPS lacks. Applying this hardware will make turn-by-turn indoor directions highly accurate.
